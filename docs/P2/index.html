Proposition: Migration Does Not Contribute to American Economic Hardship

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I visualized the full historial trends to visualize low correlation between the two features (+2)
  • I highlighted two periods: one period where CPI rates were high while migration was low, and another period where the opposite is true to clarify my proposition. I considered taking the mean of both features across two spans of time to simpify this comparison but opted for a more intuitive, clearer solution. (+1)
  • I annotated the highest CPI rate and lowest CPI rate in the visualization that both inversely trend with migration(+0.5)

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I used a bar chart to compare two years rather than overall trends to obscure the true relationship between these features. I thought about adjusting my Y-axes so they visibly trended closer but I decided that a bar chart is more powerful in its simplicity and ability to support a skewed perspective. Especially because bar charts are not a recommended chart type for time trended data. (-2)
  • I used a gradient for the CPI rate bar to emphasize the negative association with higher inflation(-1)
  • I adjusted the scale of the CPI Annual % Change Y-axis in order to emphasize the change over time (-1)
  • I annotated the visualization with the CPI rate in 1970 and 2020 to make the difference in values even more clear(-0.5)
  • I calculated the improvement in CPI rate to strengthen my narrative (-0.5)

Final Reflection

I found this project more difficult than I expected. From the World Bank database I manually pulled individual datasets for a variety of features that are often referenced in news articles, such as CPI, crime rates, immigration, employment rates, and others. I wanted to focus my submission on the United States only. Given that each dataset was organized so that each row was a country’s data, I extracted the row item for the United States in each dataset and transposed the subset so that the yearly granularity metric data was now organized as a single column. I completed this for each dataset then merged each USA filtered subset into an aggregated USA dataframe.

From here I investigated trends and I decided to focus on an issue that is frequently brought up in the news - migration and its negative impact. With my proposition that migration does not significantly impact economic hardship for Americans, I was determined to support this claim with ethical, objective chart design. In my “FOR the Proposition” chart, I wanted facts to speak for themselves. I visualized the entire dataset as a dual line chart. I selectively used annotations and highlighted key regions in this chart to make this proposition more clear. In my “AGAINST the Proposition” chart, I intentionally designed it as a bar chart to deceive the viewer that there is a positive correlation between migration and inflation. Annotations emphasize this and by using metrics I add credibility to this claim. The simplicity of the bar chart can be a powerful tool for deception because it’s so easy to interpret.

From this project, I’ve learned how important it is to use visualization features to reveal the truth objectively, rather than to manipulate data to support a personal narrative. Misleading design choices like using incorrect chart types, cherry picking data, or skewing the axes scales are inappropriate not only ethically, but also because they present data inaccurately.
